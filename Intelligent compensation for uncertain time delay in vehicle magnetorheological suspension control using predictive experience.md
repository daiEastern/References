## ABSTRACT

Aiming at the deterioration of ride comfort caused by uncertain time delay of magnetorheological (MR) damper, a feedforward-feedback collaborative mode is proposed by integrating Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) and Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) to alleviate time delay and optimize damping effect. Firstly, fuzzy Linear Quadratic Regulator algorithm is employed to simulate and control an active suspension to obtain the ideal control state information without time delay, and the LSTM is developed and trained using the ideal state information to establish the prediction model based on ideal experience; Secondly, within the Soft Actor-Critic (SAC), the prediction model is utilized to predict real-time observations, yielding predicted values for next state. Relevant experience is added to replay buffer of DRL, and the reward item of prediction error is introduced to obtain a SAC algorithm with Predictive Experience Guidance (SAC-PEG). Finally, the results of passive suspension, Proximal Policy Optimization, SAC, Twin Delayed Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient and SAC-PEG are compared by simulations and bench experiments. The simulations demonstrate that body acceleration controlled by SAC-PEG is 25.52% lower than that of passive suspension, and suspension working space and tire dynamic load are increased by 90.59% and 66.35%; Compared with SAC, when suspension working space and tire dynamic load are only deteriorated by 7.956% and 5.440%, body acceleration is optimized by 4.143%. Bench experiment also achieved satisfactory results. The results validated that SAC-PEG has better mitigation effect on uncertain time delay than other comparative methods, and can improve the smoothness problem caused by uncertain time delay.

## 1. Introduction

Against the backdrop of intelligent and high-end development in the current automotive industry, enhancing the quality of vehicle driving and riding has become one of the core competitive areas. As a key technology of intelligent chassis, semi-active suspension system can effectively reconcile the traditional contradiction between ride comfort and handling stability. And the magnetorheological (MR) damper, in its role as an intelligent executor, has become the core component of the system to achieve its functions. With millisecond level response speed, continuous and stepless damping force adjustment capability, and excellent reliability, it not only significantly improves the comprehensive performance of the suspension system, but also allows users to directly feel the significant improvement in comfort and intelligence of the vehicle in diverse driving scenarios, becoming an important way to enhance the overall driving quality of the vehicle (Abdul Aziz et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024d). To enhance the comprehensive performance of MR

suspension, extensive research has been conducted on MR dampers in terms of structural design (Goldasz et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2012), material properties (Choi, 2023; Rouabah et al., 2025), dynamic modeling (Karabulut et al., 2021; Li et al., 2025b; Savaia et al., 2021), control methods (Gad, 2020; Li et al., 2024; Mai et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2017), and response sensitivity (Yoon et al., 2021). However, in practical applications, there is an inevitable time delay or response time in the MR damper system, which remains a key bottleneck restricting the control accuracy of the system (Le et al., 2023). During vehicle operation, a long response time delay prevents the suspension from adjusting damping forces promptly and accurately according to road conditions, leading to intensified body vibrations and a substantial reduction in ride comfort. Moreover, during emergency lane changes or high-speed cornering, distorted damping force control may cause loss of body posture stability. The lag in damping force control during these critical periods poses a significant safety hazard to handling stability and driving safety of the vehicle. Therefore, effective time delay compensation for MR damper

systems with time delays has become one of the key ways to improve suspension performance and ensure driving safety (Choi et al., 2016).

The control system of MR dampers consists of multiple components, including sensors, controllers, control devices and so on. The delay of MR dampers originates from the time needed to measure the state vector response and process this data using computers. In addition, time delay is also introduced when calculating output force through control algorithms, activating control devices, and applying them to equipment or systems (Dharmajan & AlHamaydeh, 2025). Research based on extensive full-scale MR damper tests and simulations has identified three different types of time delay in MR dampers: control electronic time delays for signal acquisition, signal processing, and power supply operations; electrical time delay involving the electrical part of the damper or electromagnetic circuit, and mechanical time delays between the start of current change in the device and the beginning of mechanical behavior adjustment (Occhiuzzi et al., 2003). The time delay of MR dampers runs through the entire process of measurement, data processing, control algorithm execution, and equipment activation, among which the control electronic time delay is directly associated with the information processing and decision generation-making stages of the controller. Improving the control algorithm that considering the time delay can optimize the time efficiency of this link and reduce its constraints on the dynamic response of the system.

The existing methods for dealing with time delay problems are mainly divided into two categories: feedforward compensation and feedback correction, each with its own emphasis on principles, applicable scenarios, and limitations. Feedforward compensation generates control signals in advance by predicting the delayed system state, featuring a proactive characteristic. Theoretically, it can completely offset the impact of fixed time delay. The specific implementation approaches include: road preview compensation, which uses pre-perceived future road information combined with Model Predictive Control to respond in advance (Le et al., 2023) and compensate for delays caused by information transmission and execution (Kim et al., 2023; Xie et al., 2024); Taylor series predictive control, which designs a controller with a first-order Taylor series expansion to predict the control force considering time delay and eliminate the influence of time delay on the system (Zhang et al., 2024); Time delay stability analysis, which transforms the asymptotic stability problem of Sliding Mode Control (SMC) into a feasibility problem (Gao et al., 2023; Li et al., 2025a; Zhu et al., 2022), or uses a modified Newmark-Beta integration to solve complex motion equations of coupled structures to estimate system response (Katebi & Zadeh, 2016), further deriving time delay stability conditions and critical time delay calculation methods to enhance system stability. However, the above methods are mostly designed for fixed latency and are difficult to adapt to dynamically varying delays or system parameters. In practice, the inherent time delay characteristics of MR dampers are fundamentally dynamic rather than static, varying with operational conditions (Zand et al., 2021). Therefore, for dynamic time delay, researchers have proposed improved T-S fuzzy Smith prediction compensation controllers based on delay differential equations and Lyapunov stability criteria (Ji et al., 2025), and an  $H_\infty$  controller that uses Adaptive Neural Fuzzy Inference System (ANFIS) technology to predict command voltage input (Luu et al., 2014). However, these methods still have significant limitations in terms of strong model dependence, and the actual effectiveness of feedforward compensation methods is often limited due to the difficulty in obtaining accurate models of MR dampers.

The feedback correction scheme involves the controller acquiring feedback state information from the system and adjusting the system according to preset objectives or strategies. It does not require an accurate model and can adaptively handle time-varying delay and random disturbances. Typical schemes include: combining inverse model with backstepping control, which integrates the inverse model of the MR damper with a Taylor-series-based backstepping control strategy to calculate the required damping force of the damper through the dynamic error of the system (Long et al., 2024); Augmented Kalman Filter

Observation, using an augmented Kalman estimator to observe the real-time changes in the output force of the damper, than calculating the acceptable delay compensation predictive control force, and replacing some of the delay control force (Wang et al., 2024c); Delay dependent robust control, using cone compensation linear iteration to calculate the critical delay for system stability, designing a delay dependent  $H_\infty$  robust controller with memoryless feedback control rate, and obtaining controller feedback gain (Ye et al., 2024); Data driven methods such as fuzzy control and Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) can directly learn compensation strategies from historical data, becoming a new direction for alleviating time delay of suspension. A typical data-driven method for mitigating time delay includes creating a time delay buffer to store actions at each moment, simulating the time delay caused by measurement and response within the system based on fuzzy rules, and selecting actions as the current output according to the actual latency (Wang et al., 2024b); Simulate deterministic delay, semi-regular delay, and uncertain time delay using Twin Delayed Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient (TD3) algorithm to guide agent to obtain more robust control strategies in time-delay environments (Wang et al., 2023); Add response delay error to the tracking error of the SMC and optimize the system control parameters using reinforcement learning (Sun et al., 2022). It should be noted that there is a risk of phase lag in feedback correction, and the delay may cause the feedback signal to become outdated, leading to incorrect controller actions. The MR damper is a complex nonlinear system with multivariable coupling, and the time delay effect is also a complex nonlinear problem. DRL, with the powerful nonlinear approximation ability of Deep Neural Networks, can extract effective features from high-dimensional delayed state data and obtain global optimal or suboptimal control strategies through optimization mechanisms in time-delay environments, which is difficult for many traditional linear control theories to achieve (Wang et al., 2023). Although DRL cannot completely eliminate time delay in theory and its delay mitigation effect is weaker than feedforward compensation, its extensive application studies on MR suspension have yielded favorable control results (Yuan et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2021). In summary, feedforward compensation outperforms theoretical delay cancellation ability in fixed delay scenarios, but is limited by model dependence. Feedback correction is characterized by model independence and adaptability to time-varying scenarios, especially in data-driven methods such as DRL, which provides a powerful solution to complex nonlinear delay problems, but requires a balance between phase lag and implementation cost.

In view of the above discussion, this paper proposes a feedforward compensation and feedback correction collaborative mechanism to address the issue of reduced smoothness caused by uncertain time delays in MR suspensions. Through a feedforward compensation scheme, appropriate actions are generated in advance to suppress inappropriate action outputs in DRL and assist in the learning and adjustment process that relies on real-time output feedback. The specific method of the collaborative mechanism in this study is as follows: Firstly, different from existing fusion methods such as using LSTM to analyze and process the state space of DRL (Han et al., 2025), as a policy network in DRL algorithms (Wang et al., 2024), or embedding LSTM in the actor network of DRL algorithms (Yan et al., 2023), inspired by the deterministic experience tracking memory mechanism (Wang et al., 2024a), this study proposes a predictive experience guidance mechanism, which uses LSTM as an independent prediction module to predict damping force through complex temporal dependencies, and effectively captures the characteristics of force displacement hysteresis loop (Ferhath & Kasi, 2025). By predicting future states through historical state sequences, the generated predicted values are input into the DRL controller as part of the environment, achieving feedforward-like early action generation; Then, the DRL controller enhances its adaptability to time-varying states based on the error between real-time output and LSTM prediction. Through multiple rounds of iterative learning, it selects the optimal state as the feedback signal and dynamically adjusts the control strategy. This

process does not require an accurate model and can adapt to time-varying delays and random disturbances, suppressing inappropriate action outputs caused by feedback signal obsolescence in DRL; Finally, the implicit feedforward channel of LSTM provides a leading action reference for DRL, reducing its absolute dependence on real-time feedback and mitigating the risk of phase lag. Meanwhile, the real-time error feedback of DRL adjusts the LSTM prediction input in reverse, further enhancing the adaptability of feedforward prediction to future states. The proposed feedforward compensation and feedback correction collaborative mechanism combines the anticipatory nature of feedforward compensation with the model-free and adaptive handling of time-varying delays of feedback correction, offering broad research prospects.

The contributions of this study are manifested in the following three aspects:

- 1) Proposing a predictive experience guidance strategy: introducing the LSTM model to predict the future state of the system, and integrating these predicted information into the experience replay buffer of DRL. By utilizing the predicted values of LSTM as a reference for the state space of DRL, the algorithm can comprehensively consider the future state of system during the decision-making process, thereby actively approaching the prediction experience in the numerical dimension;
- 2) Constructing a feedforward compensation and feedback correction collaborative architecture: integrating the predictive capabilities of the LSTM model with the real-time feedback characteristics of DRL. By calculating the difference between the predicted values of LSTM and the next state in DRL, and incorporating this difference into the reward function as a penalty term for prediction error, the agent is encouraged to learn accurate delay compensation strategies. This enhances the ability of the agent to predict future state and reducing time delay in the time dimension;
- 3) Designing a novel scheme of cross-system knowledge transfer: although active suspension and MR suspension have differences in structure and working principle, both aim to achieve damping function in suspension systems. This study proposes leveraging vibration damping experience data from active suspensions to pre-train the LSTM model, enabling knowledge transfer and sharing across different suspension systems. This scheme not only provides abundant data support and prior knowledge for MR suspension vibration damping training, but also broadens the data sources and application scope of the algorithm.

## 3. Delay resolution algorithm

### 3.1. Overall framework of feedforward compensation and feedback correction collaborative strategy

The feedback mechanism of DRL controlling MR suspension is crucial for improving the performance of the suspension system. This feedback mechanism passively alleviates the delay problem of suspension control by continuously sensing the state of the suspension system, selecting the optimal control action, evaluating the control effect, and adjusting the strategy. The feedback mechanism process for controlling the MR suspension is shown in Fig. 4. When the vehicle is driving on uneven roads, the road excitation will be transmitted to the suspension and tires. At this time, the agent of DRL can obtain the current state information of the MR suspension, including body acceleration, suspension working space, and tire dynamic load. In the DRL algorithm, the Actor network selects the optimal control action based on the current state and a preset reward function, and determines the required value of control current through the inverse model of the MR suspension. The reward function is designed based on the performance indicators of the suspension system to encourage algorithms to select control actions that can improve suspension performance. Send the selected optimal control action to the damper, and the damper adjusts the damping force based on the control action. After the control action is applied to the MR suspension, the suspension will enter a new state under the excitation of the road environment. Retrieve the status information of the suspension system again and evaluate the effectiveness of the control actions. The evaluation process is as follows: Firstly, the Critic networks in DRL evaluate the value of the action generated by the Actor network in the current state, that is, the agent receives corresponding reward, which reflect the degree of improvement of the suspension system performance by the current control action; Secondly, the agent stores the current state, action taken, reward obtained, and the next state as an experience sample in the replay buffer; Then, during the training process, the DRL algorithm will randomly sample a batch of experience samples from the replay buffer. Based on these experience samples, the Actor network and Critic will update according to the loss functions. Actor network adjusts its own parameters to generate control actions that can receive higher reward; The Critic networks evaluate the value of actions more accurately by updating parameters; Finally, after multiple interactions and training, the Actor network of the agent gradually learns the optimal control strat-

egy, which can select the most suitable control current based on different road excitation and suspension states, thus achieving precise control of the MR suspension.

However, in practical environments, when applying DRL algorithms to MR suspensions, the delay in current response and particle recombination of MR fluids are difficult to avoid. Therefore, this study constructs a feedforward compensation and feedback correction collaborative architecture, combining LSTM with time series prediction ability with DRL. Using LSTM model to predict the next state of MR suspension, providing feedforward signal for DRL, alleviating current response delay and MR particle recombination delay. And the prediction error is used as a penalty term in the reward function to force the DRL strategy to align with the "ideal response" of the LSTM.

The overall process of the feedforward compensation and feedback correction collaborative mechanism framework that integrates LSTM and DRL is shown in Fig. 5. Specifically, when creating a fuzzy Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR) model and an active suspension model, it should be noted that the modeling of active suspension does not consider the time delay of the active suspension actuator, and only analyzes the dynamic relationship under ideal conditions. Therefore, the empirical data of active suspension does not have the influence of time delay, nor does it introduce additional time delay burden for MR suspension. After exchanging information between the active suspension and the road environment, the state information of the active suspension is collected, which will serve as a reference for subsequent optimization and be used to measure the possible optimization effect of the MR damper. However, due to hardware limitations, MR dampers cannot fully achieve the ideal optimal damping effect mentioned above. Therefore, an LSTM model is created to analyze the state information of active suspension and extract empirical data that can reflect the optimal working state of the suspension system. These empirical data are used to train the LSTM model and construct the optimal experience library. Then, during the interaction between the MR damper and the environment, real-time data of state is collected. Input these state data into the trained LSTM model, and the model predicts the optimal prediction values for the next state based on the current state. At the same time, in the reward setting of DRL algorithm, a prediction error term is added, which is the difference between the optimal prediction value and the actual state value of the next step in the algorithm. In each round and step of the training process in the algorithm, the experience containing predicted state and the reward for the calculated prediction error term will be added to the replay buffer. Through each round and step of training, the agent can continuously adjust its control strategy based on the data in the replay buffer, gradually reducing the difference between the actual value and the predicted value to improve the damping performance of the MR damper.

### 3.2. Predictive experience playback guidance and new scheme for cross system knowledge transfer

LSTM, as an important variant of Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), can effectively capture long-range dependencies in long sequence data through cell state and gating mechanism, overcoming the gradient vanishing or exploding problems of traditional RNNs. Its gating mechanism can dynamically control the flow of information, and flexibly determine the retention, forgetting, or output of information based on input data and current state. With these advantages, LSTM performs well in time series forecasting tasks such as stock price prediction and weather forecasting. It can capture long-term trends and periodic changes in historical data, improving prediction accuracy.

In the field of suspension control, the signals generated by the suspension system have continuity, and their state variables have complex coupling relationships. When implementing DRL control, the output of the algorithm significantly depends on the previous state of the system, which means that time series data with long-range dependencies needs to be processed. The outstanding ability of LSTM in processing such data enables it to be well applied in DRL control of suspension systems, helping the agent better understand changes in system states and develop better control strategies.

To better handle the time sequence characteristics of state variables of the suspension system, an LSTM model is created to predict the next state of suspension. As shown in Fig. 6, LSTM adjusts the current input  $X_t$ , the previous hidden layer state  $h_{t-1}$ , and the historical hidden state  $C_{t-1}$  through a forget gate, input gate, and output gate structure. This regulatory mechanism enables information to selectively influence the state at each moment in the network, thereby generating new hidden states and effectively capturing temporal dependencies in sequential data.

By pre-training LSTM to provide more ideal prediction of next state for DRL, the strategy learning process of agent can be accelerated, and the agent can quickly eliminate actions that cause the state to deviate from the optimal result, thereby avoiding ineffective exploration.

### 3.3. Prediction error penalty

The state space and action space of the MR suspension damping optimization model based on DRL are designed as  $S_t = [\ddot{z}_b(t), z_a(t) - z_b(t), k_a q(t) - z_a(t)]$  and  $A = [F_{MR}]$ , respectively, where  $F_{MR}$  represents the required damping force for MR suspension.

Considering that vehicle acceleration has a significant impact on vertical smoothness, followed by suspension working space and tire dynamic load, the primary goal is to improve vehicle smoothness. Body acceleration is given a greater reward weight, while suspension working space and tire dynamic load are supplemented by safety considerations, with the latter two given a smaller reward weight. Add a penalty term to the reward function for suspension working space exceeding the safety threshold, with a weight of 100. Therefore, the reward function is set as follows:

$$r_{performance} = -(k_1 \ddot{z}_b^2(t) + k_2(z_b(t) - z_a(t))^2 + k_3 k_a^2(z_a(t) - q(t))^2 + k_4 E_s^2) \quad (3)$$

Where,  $k_1 \sim k_4$  represent the reward weight of the vehicle body acceleration, the suspension working space, the tire dynamic load, and the item of exceeding the safety threshold, respectively. This study introduces a prediction error penalty term in the reward function, which is the difference between the next state predicted by LSTM and the actual next state. By minimizing the prediction error, DRL can guide the agent to learn a state transition strategy that is closer to the optimal experience of active suspension without time delay, thereby making the algorithm "actively" reduce time delay. The equation for the penalty term of prediction error is

$$r_{prediction} = -(\lambda_1 (\ddot{z}_b^p(t+1) - \ddot{z}_b(t+1))^2 + \lambda_2 (z_a^p(t+1) - z_a(t+1))^2 + \lambda_3 k_a^2 (z_a^p(t+1) - q(t+1))^2) \quad (4)$$

Where,  $\ddot{z}_b^p(t+1)$ ,  $z_a^p(t+1)$ ,  $z_b^p(t+1)$  and  $k_a(z_a^p(t+1) - q(t+1))$  are the next state predicted by LSTM for the body acceleration, suspension working space, and tire dynamic load of the MR suspension, respectively.  $\lambda_1$ ,  $\lambda_2$  and  $\lambda_3$  are the weights of the three state prediction errors.

Therefore, the overall reward function is

$$r_t = r_{performance} + r_{prediction} \quad (5)$$

### 3.4. Introduction of SAC algorithm guided by predictive experience

SAC is a DRL algorithm based on the maximum entropy framework, which integrates off policy learning and Actor-Critic architecture, aiming to improve the robustness and sample efficiency of policies by balancing exploration and utilization. The core goal of SAC is to maximize the entropy of the strategy while maximizing cumulative rewards, encouraging the strategy to take diverse actions in the same state and avoiding premature convergence to suboptimal solutions. Therefore, the overall reward function is

$$J(\pi) = \sum_{t=0}^{T} E_{(s_t, a_t) \sim \rho_\pi} [(r(s_t, a_t) + \alpha H(\pi(\cdot|s_t)))] \quad (6)$$

Where  $J(\pi)$  is the cumulative reward;  $H(\pi(\cdot|s_t))$  is the entropy regularization term introduced by the SAC algorithm to represent the randomness of the policy; The temperature coefficient  $\alpha$  is the weight that controls entropy, used to balance the priority of exploration and utilization.

SAC consists of an Actor network and two sets of Critic networks. The goal of the Actor network is to maximize the expected return and entropy of a policy, with a loss function of  $L_\pi(\theta) = E_{s \sim R, a \sim \pi_\theta} [\alpha \log(\pi_\theta(a|s)) - Q(s, a)]$ . The Actor network outputs the mean  $\mu(s)$  and logarithmic standard deviation  $\log \sigma(s)$  of a Gaussian distribution and samples the action of  $a = \mu(s) + \sigma(s) * \lambda$ ,  $\lambda \sim N(0, 1)$  using reparameterization trick. Each set of Critic networks contains two Q-networks. When updating, the network with the smaller Q-value is selected to alleviate the problem of overestimation. When calculating its target Q-value, an entropy term is introduced:

$$y_i = r_i + \gamma (\min_{i=1,2} Q_i^{\text{target}}(s_{t+1}, a_{t+1}) - \alpha \log \pi(a_{t+1}|s_{t+1})) \quad (7)$$

Where  $\gamma$  is the discount factor.

Update Critic Network by Minimizing Bellman Error:

$$L = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{2} (y_i - Q_i(s, a))^2 \quad (8)$$

In order to achieve high returns for the strategy as much as possible, while ensuring that the average entropy level of the strategy is not lower than the target entropy, the Lagrange multiplier method is used to transform this constraint into a learnable loss function about  $\alpha$ :

$$L(\alpha) = E_{s \sim R, a \sim \pi} [-\alpha \log \pi(a|s) - \alpha H_0] \quad (9)$$

# 6. Conclusion and prospect

In response to the time-delay issue in the actuator of the MR damper, a collaborative architecture combining feedforward compensation and feedback correction is established. This method utilizes LSTM networks to generate advanced signals for the system damping task and employs DRL to adjust system errors in real time, thereby reducing deviations in both the time dimension and the vibration damping performance dimension. Through simulation experiments and bench tests of passive suspension and MR suspension, the following conclusions can be drawn:

- 1) Verify the nonlinear relationship between time delay and the vibration response of MR suspension through simulation experiments: after introducing the set time delay, the RMS of the suspension body acceleration based on DRL shows a monotonically increasing characteristic, and its increase amplitude also increases;
- 2) Under the designed bump road excitation, the improved method proposed in this study shortens the response time of the MR suspension to impact disturbances, validating the effectiveness of the delay compensation strategy: detailed enlarged views of the time domain response results for body acceleration, as well as a comparison chart of vehicle body acceleration from bench experiments, demonstrate that the SAC-PEG algorithm, guided by predictive experience, responds to vibrations ahead of time in the time dimension. These analyses reveal that a collaborative compensation architecture integrating the feedforward prediction capability of LSTM with the feedback correction capability of DRL is more advantageous in mitigating the time delay effects in MR suspensions.
- 3) The SAC-PEG control algorithm proposed in this study demonstrates significant advantages in vibration optimization: SAC-PEG has a good inhibitory effect on deterministic time delay, and in the simulation and bench experiments of controlling uncertain time delay, the RMS of vertical acceleration for the MR suspension controlled by SAC-PEG is 25.52% and 52.34% respectively lower than that of the passive suspension, and 4.143% and 5.881% respectively lower than that controlled by the SAC algorithm, significantly enhancing ride comfort.

In the future, this study will further research in the following directions to promote the application of improved algorithms in practical scenarios:

- 1) When using DRL algorithms for training, each step requires calculating the state prediction error, which increases the complexity of the algorithm. In the future, we can explore how to set more efficient strategies to encourage agents to learn delay compensation;
- 2) In practical applications, it is necessary to design multi-objective optimization algorithms to adapt to different road surfaces and requirements by comprehensively considering vehicle acceleration, suspension dynamic deflection, and suspension dynamic load.
