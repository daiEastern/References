## ABSTRACT

The ammonia-fueled Wankel rotary engine represents a promising power solution balancing performance and emissions. Oxy-combustion significantly enhances combustion, enabling ammonia-fueled engines to achieve favorable combustion characteristics. In the present work, rich and lean combustion are comparatively analyzed in ammonia-oxygen Wankel rotary engines to understand their respective advantages. Key findings are summarized as follows: Under the test conditions, the flammable limit of lean combustion exceeds 5.0 excess oxygen ratio, while that of rich combustion is 0.5 excess oxygen ratio. The maximum indicated mean effective pressure will be achieved at the lean and rich side, respectively 1.07 and 1.30 MPa, rather than the stoichiometric ratio. Existing spark plug configurations are suboptimal for ammonia-oxygen operation, necessitating redesign of aperture geometry and positioning. Both rich and lean combustion exhibit reduced flame propagation velocity and combustion efficiency at higher deviations from the stoichiometric ratio. Rich combustion has higher peak pressure, temperature and indicated mean effective pressure, however, as well as significantly lower combustion efficiency. In terms of emissions, although rich combustion has advantages in all nitrogen oxides components, its unburned ammonia emissions are significantly poorer than lean combustion.

## 1. Introduction

With increasing concern about global warming, many economies have jointly formulated a series of measures to control global carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>) emissions [1]. Internal combustion engines (ICEs) are key power devices in the fields of vehicles, ship propulsion, power generation, rail traction, etc., and they still rely mainly on fossil fuels, which bear important responsibility for global high CO<sub>2</sub> emissions [2]. Except for enhancing the performance of existing ICEs [3], the application of carbon-free fuels is one of the major measures for decarbonizing ICEs, such as methanol [4], hydrogen [5] and ammonia [6].

Except for carbon-free emission, ammonia also has some advantages, such as high energy density, high octane number, carbon-free hydrogen carrier, and convenient storage and transportation [7]. Specifically, ammonia, as a renewable energy, has abundant sources and mature preparation technologies [8]. Ammonia has also been proven to be a

potential alternative fuel choice for ICEs. The application of ammonia in ICEs can be traced back to the research of Norsk in 1905, and many companies have hitherto shown strong interest in ammonia-fueled ICEs [9]. However, different from hydrogen [10], high ignition energy, low flammability [11], slow burning velocity [12] and low volumetric heat value [13] result in poor combustion and power performance in ammonia-fueled ICEs. Cornelius et al. [14] identified that the inherently low reactivity of ammonia necessitates ignition timing typically exceeding 100°CA after top dead center (ATDC) in ammonia-fueled ICEs. These engines also exhibit approximately 12 % lower indicated thermal efficiency (ITE) compared to gasoline-fueled ICEs of similar specifications. In addition, a turbocharging ammonia-fueled ICE with an 11.5 compression ratio (CR) only achieves similar power to the gasoline-fueled ICE with 9.4 CR [15]. In particular, since ammonia contains nitrogen element, the formation mechanism of nitrogen oxides (NO<sub>x</sub>) in ammonia-fueled ICEs is more complex than that in hydrogen-fueled ICEs [16], as well as higher NO<sub>x</sub> emissions. Poor combustion performance additionally leads to substantial unburned ammonia emissions in ammonia-fueled ICEs. Mounaim-Rousselle et al. [18] concluded that the ammonia concentration in the exhaust gas of ammonia-fueled ICEs exceeds 3 %. Collectively, while ammonia represents a potential ICE fuel, persistent challenges in power density, combustion efficiency, and nitrogen-based emissions require resolution in ammonia-fueled ICEs.

The Wankel rotary engine (WRE) exhibits high power density and compactness relative to conventional internal combustion engines (ICEs) [19]. Compared to four-stroke reciprocating piston engines (RPEs) with identical compression ratios (CR) and fuel, gasoline-fueled WREs generate 50 % greater power per liter, while hydrogen-fueled versions achieve 66 % higher power density [20]. Moreover, WREs demonstrate exceptional fuel flexibility, accommodating low-carbon alternatives including methane [21], methanol [22], syngas [23], hydrogen [24], ammonia [25], and so on with minor modifications. Considering the low reactivity of ammonia, its current application in WRE is usually accompanied by hydrogen [26]. Meng et al. [17] experimentally found that ammonia-hydrogen dual fuel operation in WREs delivers superior efficiency to pure hydrogen across wide ammonia volumetric fractions. Wang et al. [27] demonstrated the flammability of ammonia in WRE and found that ammonia is almost nonflammable when the hydrogen substitution ratio is less than 5 %, while ammonia can be completely burned when the hydrogen substitution ratio exceeds 12.5 %. Wang et al. [28] found that although lean combustion contributes to the improvement of ammonia-hydrogen dual fuel WRE thermal efficiency, limited by the low flammability of ammonia, the excess air ratio closer to the stoichiometric ratio has a more stable initial flame kernel. Pan et al. concluded that injection position [25], injection timing [29] and engine speed [29] play important roles in the performance of ammonia-hydrogen dual fuel WRE. Collectively, WRE technology shows strong potential for mitigating ammonia's power density limitations. While pure ammonia-fueled WREs remain unreported, ammonia's inherent properties suggest persistent challenges, including ignition difficulty and low combustion efficiency. Consequently, combustion enhancement strategies (e.g., hydrogen blending) constitute critical research priorities for ammonia-fueled WRE development.

Oxy-combustion is an advanced combustion technology that enhances fuel combustion by increasing the oxygen content in the air [30], demonstrating efficacy in improving the performance of engines fueled with gasoline [31], diesel [32], methanol [33], hydrogen [34], etc. At present, however, there is relatively little research on the application of oxy-combustion in ammonia-fueled ICEs. Ji et al. [35] reported significant brake mean effective pressure (BMEP) improvement and reduced cyclic variation in ammonia-hydrogen dual-fuel RPE using oxy-combustion. Park et al. [36] observed enhanced efficiency, reduced cycle-to-cycle variation, and decreased nitrous oxide (N<sub>2</sub>O) and

unburned ammonia emissions in direct-injection ammonia-fueled RPEs when oxygen volume fraction increased from 21 % to 30 %. In terms of ammonia oxy-combustion research, Huang et al. [37] found that with the increase of oxygen content, the participation ability of H, O and OH in chemical reactions increases, and the generation rate of N-related intermediate substances such as NH<sub>2</sub>, NH, N, and NHO significantly increases, thus promoting the combustion of ammonia. Zhong et al. [38] concluded that the high burning temperature caused by oxy-combustion can compensate for the heat dissipation of ammonia flame under turbulent conditions. Wang et al. [39] demonstrated that high oxygen content enhances the resistance of ammonia flame to turbulent wrinkles. However, high oxygen availability and burning temperature under oxy-combustion further promote the increase of NO<sub>x</sub> emissions generated by ammonia combustion [40]. Critically, NO<sub>x</sub> formation pathways exhibit strong sensitivity to both equivalence ratio and oxygen enrichment level [41]. Ji et al. [42] found that the variation of nitric oxide (NO), as the main NO<sub>x</sub> produced by ammonia combustion, has divergent behaviors under rich and lean combustion conditions. Based on the above analysis, these findings position oxy-combustion as a promising approach for mitigating ammonia's characteristically poor combustion in WREs, though NO<sub>x</sub> trade-offs warrant careful optimization.

Collectively, WRE offers potential to mitigate ammonia's power density limitations, while oxy-combustion enhances ammonia combustion in WRE. Critically, ammonia synthesized from electrolytic hydrogen pairs synergistically with co-produced oxygen for combustion enhancement, establishing ammonia-oxygen ICEs as significant carbon-neutral power solutions. Nevertheless, fundamental combustion mechanisms of ammonia-oxygen mixtures in WRE and even ICE remain inadequately characterized. Specifically, the excess oxygen ratio is an important parameter affecting the reaction pathway, and there is a research gap in its impacts on ammonia-oxygen WRE. Based on the above considerations, this work intends to systematically investigate the combustion and emission characteristics of ammonia-oxygen in the WRE across a wide excess oxygen ratio (0.1–5.0), with particular emphasis on comparative analysis of rich and lean combustion of ammonia-oxygen. The present work simultaneously illustrates the combustion and emission characteristics of an ammonia-oxygen WRE under rich and lean combustion conditions, while revealing the advantages and disadvantages of each combustion mode. It thereby provides theoretical guidance for the application of ammonia-oxygen WREs and even ICEs in general.

## 2. Engine introduction and model establishment

The present work aims to investigate the difference between rich combustion and lean combustion of ammonia under pure oxygen conditions in the WRE. This work advances ammonia-oxygen turbulent combustion research while providing theoretical guidance for ammonia-oxygen application in WRE and even ICEs. Especially, due to the removal of the dilution effect of nitrogen on the in-cylinder charge, the

ammonia-oxygen WRE can achieve higher power than stoichiometric-combustion hydrogen-fueled WRE and even gasoline-fueled WRE with air under a wide range of excess oxygen ratio conditions, as shown in Fig. 1. In the legend of Fig. 1, ST represents the operation is at stoichiometric ratio.

This study employs numerical simulations to investigate an ammonia-oxygen WRE under port injection (PI), spark ignition (SI), 3000 r/min and wide-open throttle (WOT) conditions. In addition, all ignition timings are fixed at top dead center (TDC) to ensure the unique variable principle. For rich combustion, the excess oxygen ratio is parametrically varied from 0.1 to 1.0 at intervals of 0.1, while for lean combustion, the excess oxygen ratio ranges from 1.0 to 5.0 at intervals of 0.4, with stoichiometric combustion included in both series. The main research content of this work is divided into two parts. The first part independently evaluates lean and rich combustion, and the second part comparatively assesses the characteristics of rich and lean combustion. In the present work, the construction of geometric models and experimental verification are based on the Mazda 13B WRE. This WRE is dual-cylinder, water-cooled, naturally aspirated and side-intake and exhaust. The specification of applied WRE is shown in Table 1 and the schematic diagram of WRE is shown in Fig. 2. Notably, to reduce cylinder-to-cylinder leakage and ensure stable ignition, Mazda designed different hole diameters for the leading spark plug (LSP) and trailing spark plug (TSP) in the 13B WRE. Therefore, the flame propagation velocity in the spark plug holes are not the same, which will be quantitatively analyzed in Section 3.

Numerical simulations were conducted using CONVERGE computational fluid dynamics (CFD) software. To ensure the reliability of the simulation, based on previous work, some models are used in the simulation model of this work. RNG k- $\epsilon$  model is selected as the turbulent flow model [43]; the SAGE combustion model is chosen as the combustion model [44]; a reduced ammonia reaction kinetics mechanism by Okafor is applied as the chemical reaction kinetics mechanism [45]; O'Rourke and Amsden model is used as the heat transfer model [46]; spark-energy deposition model is applied to achieve the ignition [47]. The detailed models are shown in Table 2. To guarantee the high accuracy and low calculated time cost, the model adopts a 2 mm based grid size, as well as applying adaptive mesh refinement (AMR) to the entire combustion region. In addition, some boundary conditions, such as intake and exhaust temperature, are listed in Table 3.

In ammonia-fueled ICEs,  $\text{NO}_x$  comprises thermal  $\text{NO}_x$  and fuel  $\text{NO}_x$  [15]. Although ammonia-oxygen WRE eliminates atmospheric nitrogen, nitrogen from ammonia combustion still forms thermal  $\text{NO}_x$  with pure oxygen combustion's elevated temperatures particularly promoting its formation [54]. Thermal  $\text{NO}_x$  is generated by post-oxidation in high-temperature burned regions, while fuel  $\text{NO}_x$  is generated in large quantities at the flame front and further oxidized to nitrogen in the burned region. Fig. 10 illustrates the profile of NO mass against the crank angle at varied excess oxygen ratios. It can be found that lean combustion exhibits significantly higher maximum and export NO mass compared to stoichiometric combustion, as well as a lower post-oxidation rate. This elevated NO stems primarily from enriched O/H radical pools, while diminished post-oxidation results from lower burned-zone temperatures. According to the Zeldovich mechanism [55], temperature is the most important factor in the generation of thermal  $\text{NO}_x$ . As shown in Fig. 6b, temperatures decrease with rising excess oxygen ratios, eventually becoming insufficient to sustain thermal NO formation. The formation of thermal NO affects the post-oxidation of fuel NO [56], therefore, a higher maximum NO is obtained when the thermal NO is no longer generated. However, as the excess oxygen ratio is further increased, the reduction of burning velocity and combustion efficiency leads to a gradual decrease in maximum NO.

Fig. 11 illustrates the flame propagation with the crank angle across varying excess oxygen ratios. Because the flame at 0.1 and 0.2 excess

oxygen ratios exhibit similar characteristics to those at 0.3 excess oxygen ratio, they are not presented in Fig. 11. It can be found that the flame propagation is slowed down as the excess oxygen ratio is increased, especially for low excess oxygen ratios. At 0.4 excess oxygen ratio, the flame generated by TSP is gradually extinguished by in-cylinder turbulence as the rotor rotates, and when the excess oxygen ratio is 0.3, the flame generated by both spark plugs is quenched and can not propagate into the combustion chamber. These observations establish 0.5 excess oxygen ratio as the rich flammability limit for ammonia-oxygen WRE under test conditions. In addition, cross-referencing Fig. 5 and Fig. 11 reveals that maximum flame propagation velocity occurs at stoichiometric conditions, with both lean and rich combustion diminishing propagation velocity.

As shown in Fig. 12, both pressure and temperature are greatly decreased as the excess oxygen ratio is decreased, as well as retarded corresponding crank angle. Especially for the excess oxygen ratio of 0.5, its pressure and temperature are significantly decreased compared to other high excess oxygen ratios, which can also be reflected in Fig. 11b. When the excess oxygen ratio is reduced from 1.0 to 0.5, the peak pressure is decreased from 9.09 to 2.38 MPa and the peak temperature is decreased from 3407 to 1996 K.

Fig. 17a shows combustion parameters of rich and lean combustion at varied mean pressure rising rates, and it can be found that rich and lean combustion with the same mean pressure rising rate have similar CA10, CA50 and CA90, validating mean pressure rising rate as a reliable combustion criterion. Fig. 17b illustrates the peak in-cylinder pressure and temperature of rich and lean combustion at varied mean pressure rising rates. As illustrated in Fig. 17b, while peak pressure and temperature show minimal divergence at low mean pressure rising rates, rich combustion exhibits significantly higher values than lean combustion at elevated rates. This means that rich combustion has a higher thermal load [59] than lean combustion, however, this difference is gradually decreased as the charge deviates from the stoichiometric ratio.

Fig. 18 compares IMEP and combustion efficiency of rich and lean combustion at varied mean pressure rising rates. It can be concluded that rich combustion consistently yields higher IMEP than lean combustion at the same mean pressure rising rate, indicating that rich combustion has a stronger power output at the same burning velocity. This is mainly due to the higher in-cylinder heat value at rich combustion. For the combustion efficiency, limited by the oxygen concentration, rich combustion has an obviously lower combustion efficiency than lean combustion at the same mean pressure rising rate. Crucially, comparing the standardized combustion efficiency of rich combustion and combustion efficiency of lean combustion, it can be found that standardized rich combustion efficiency aligns closely with lean combustion efficiency across most pressure rising rates, indicating that whether it is rich or lean combustion, their utilization rates of scarce substances (oxygen in rich combustion and ammonia in lean combustion) are similar and remain at a relatively high level.

# 4. Conclusions

This work comparatively analyzes the rich and lean combustion characteristics of ammonia-oxygen WRE through numerical simulations at 3000 r/min, WOT and TDC ignition timing. The excess oxygen ratio investigated in the present work spans 0.1–1.0 for rich combustion and 1.0–5.0 for lean combustion. Key findings are:

1. In lean combustion, increasing the excess oxygen ratio alters combustion characteristics. Slow burning velocities at high excess oxygen ratios prevent flame propagation backward or even being blown away from the spark plug. When the excess oxygen ratio is increased from 1.0 to 5.0, the peak pressure and temperature are decreased from 9.09 to 1.55 MPa and 3407 to 1606 K, respectively, as well as an absolute about 14 % reduction of combustion efficiency. Lean combustion affects rapid combustion periods more significantly than ignition delays. In particular, although all combustion pressures perform positive work, the 1.8 excess oxygen ratio has the highest IMEP, about 1.07 MPa, at the test ignition timing. Emissions analysis reveals unburned ammonia decreases then increases with leaner mixtures, while  $\text{NO}_x$  shows the inverse trend – both reaching extrema at 2.6 excess oxygen ratio (min  $\text{NH}_3$ ) and 3.4 (max  $\text{NO}_x$ ) excess oxygen ratio. NO dominates  $\text{NO}_x$  emissions, with post-oxidation capability improving at higher excess oxygen ratios.
2. For rich combustion, under tested conditions, the flame will be extinguished by turbulence and even quenched in the spark plug hole at a low excess oxygen ratio, establishing 0.5 excess oxygen ratio as the practical flammability limit. Decreasing the excess oxygen ratio from 1.0 to 0.5 reduces peak pressure from 9.09 to 2.38 MPa and temperature from 3407 to 1996 K, as well as an absolute 57 % reduction of combustion efficiency and a 7 % reduction of standardized combustion efficiency. Rich combustion impacts the rapid combustion period more than ignition delay, and the highest IMEP is obtained at 0.8 excess oxygen ratio, about 1.30 MPa. In terms of emission, unburned ammonia mass rises by 5 orders of magnitude as the excess oxygen ratio is decreased from 1.0 to 0.5, while  $\text{NO}_x$  reduces by 3 orders of magnitude. NO is the main  $\text{NO}_x$  component and the NO post-oxidation ability of rich combustion is better than that of stoichiometric combustion.
3. Original engine spark plug holes designed for gasoline are proven unsuitable for ammonia-oxygen combustion. Oversized LSP holes impede flame propagation. TSP position requires optimization to ensure robust flame propagation in qualitative-control ammonia-oxygen WRE.
4. The mean pressure rising rate from ignition timing to the crank angle of peak pressure is applied as the comparison metric. Rich combustion generates significantly higher peak in-cylinder pressures/temperatures than lean combustion at high pressure rising rates, with minimal differences at low rates. Rich combustion achieves higher IMEP but lower combustion efficiency at equivalent pressure rising rates. However, whether rich or lean combustion, their utilization rates of scarce substances (oxygen in rich combustion, ammonia in lean combustion) remain at a high level. In addition, rich combustion has poor unburned ammonia emission, however, substantially lower  $\text{N}_2\text{O}$ ,  $\text{NO}_2$  and NO emission than lean combustion at the same mean pressure rising rate.

Collectively, rich and lean combustion exert distinct influences on ammonia-oxygen WRE performance. Lean combustion enables stable operation across wider flammability ranges. In addition, lean conditions offer advantages in combustion efficiency and unburned ammonia control, while rich combustion enhances power density and reduces  $\text{NO}_x$  emissions.
