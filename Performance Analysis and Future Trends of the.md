**Abstract.** As the global focus shifts towards environmental protection and sustainable energy development, electric vehicles are increasingly seen as the future of transportation. However, internal combustion engines (ICEs) continue to offer various irreplaceable advantages, particularly in terms of cost-effectiveness and reliability. The Mazda Wankel rotary engine, in spite of a lower compression ratio compared to traditional reciprocating engines, stands out for its compact size, lightweight design, and ability to deliver high horsepower with rapid operation. This unique combination of features makes the Wankel engine a noteworthy alternative to conventional ICEs. This paper will delve into the performance characteristics of the Mazda Wankel rotary engine, highlighting its strengths and limitations. Additionally, the study will explore the potential future trends for this engine technology, considering ongoing advancements and market demands. The analysis aims to provide a comprehensive understanding of the Wankel engine's role in the automotive industry amidst the growing prominence of electric vehicles.

## 1 Introduction

Mazda Wankel Engines are renowned in the automotive industry for their distinctive design and exceptional performance. Unlike conventional piston engines, rotor engines harness power through rotational motion, granting them significant advantages in terms of compactness, lightweight construction, and high revolution per minute (RPM) capabilities. Since the 1960s, Mazda has consistently applied this innovative engine technology to numerous iconic models, such as the acclaimed RX-7 and RX-8 series, continuously advancing and refining its technical principles while tracing a rich historical development.

Furthermore, the Wankel Rotary Engine needs some technique improvements to eliminate its inevitable shortcomings. Since it has been founded for decades, however, in the global background of emission reduction, the problems of the Wankel Rotary Engine are relatively low combustion efficiency, high fuel consumption, and unburnt hydrocarbon emissions, attributed to constraints on flame speed, elongated combustion chambers, and low compression ratios [1]. Nevertheless, new technology like the hydrogen-fueled Wankel rotary engine can achieve zero emission [2]. After solving the main problem, the improved

## 2 Technical principles

### 2.1 Working principles

Felix Heinrich Wankel, a German mechanical engineer, designed the Wankel rotary engine. This engine is a type of pistonless four-stroke internal combustion engine, which is the four cycles of the Otto engine without a piston. Instead of a piston, Wankel uses an almost-triangular(trochoidal) rotor moving around in an almost-elliptical(epitrochoidal) housing [3].

The three tips of the rotor are slid along the walls of the chamber so that during a single complete revolution of the rotor, the air inside is divided into two segments. The larger gear is on one side of the rotor, and it is attached to the tips. They are meant to keep the rotor centered. The rotor has, on one side, toothed gears for hula-hoop rotation to keep it from sliding down; on the other hand, the teeth are not engaged in the act of wobbling or dynamic repositioning but are there solely to keep the rotor in phase with the air. In addition, specifically, a plain Babbitt-style bearing allows for the normal rotor rotation caused by the eccentric shaft, which goes through the small gear and functions as an output shaft. The eccentric shaft takes three cycles along its trajectory in the casing for a single rotor revolution, which happens once.

The combined use of the eccentric shaft and the constancy or "timing" in the rotation of the rotor freezes the relationship between the two tips; thus, the tips form a path known as an epitrochoid, which has a complex equation but looks like an oval with a pinched hole. The rotor tips revolve in such a way as to form the mentioned chambers, each having a different volume for every single rotation, making this rotor an excellent design for propulsion and fluid movement in compressors, corkscrew pumps, etc. The three chambers will change from the intake, then compression, later expansion, to the last exhaust phase. The first representation shows, inside A, the incoming air through the open intake port while the engine is overcoming the intake cycle; the entire system is inhaling a new mixture. At the tightest situation, which is the maximum compression, chamber B is burning the mixture to generate power. Being at about  $30^\circ$  of the intake phase, chamber C would be on the exhaust phase. The Fig.1 shows the entire working process of the Wankel Rotary Engine.

### 2.2 Power comparison

The working process of Wankel rotary engines is a four-stroke cycle, which is the same as the reciprocating engines. As Fig. 2 shows, it needs two crankshaft revolutions ( $720^\circ$  CA-Crank Angle) for a reciprocating engine to fulfill a four-stroke cycle. However, every three revolutions at each rotor revolution ( $1080^\circ$  EA-Eccentric Angle) completed by an eccentric shaft of the Wankel rotary engine will finish three four-stroke cycles in total since each side of the rotor revolution fulfills each four-stroke cycle [4]. Since the number of the moving parts of the chamber which used to combust is decreased, it can achieve a higher RPM than traditional piston motor. Thus, each power output created by a Wankel rotary engine can be seen as three times that of a reciprocating engine.

One of the main limitations is related to the design of the rotor sealing system, specifically regarding the apex seal [5]. In this scenario, a single slat is responsible for fulfilling the role typically carried out by piston rings in a conventional engine. Moreover, unfavorable variations in the angle at which the apex seal operates hinder achieving an optimal shape for its sliding surface concerning oil film parameters. As a result, this leads to a decline in mating conditions between elements of the apex seal, ultimately resulting in reduced efficiency and longevity.

### 2.3 Hydrogen power source

Research on the application of both quantitative and qualitative control of a hydrogen-fueled Wankel rotary engine (WRE) and look for a proper load control strategy that is expected to improve upon current drawbacks [6]. If the operation is carried out at 1500 revolutions per minute, then hydrogen-fueled WREs have a greater efficiency. Hydrogen-fueled WREs have their own shortcomings in comparison to hydrogen-fueled reciprocating engines; these include low brake thermal efficiency, uneven thermal load, high knock numbers, and high NOx emissions as well. The hybrid control (qualitative control combined with some elements of quantitative control) can noticeably increase brake thermal efficiency, reduce the thermal imbalance around the combustion side, reduce NO emission, and reduce the knocking tendency to some extent, with a moderate penalty on power stability. This hybrid load control is also capable of selecting appropriate matching logic depending on the priority order, including power, efficiency, emission, and stability. Brake thermal efficiency of the load control method of 38.4% (on the relative basis) and 4.75% (on the absolute basis) in the operation area. Moreover, 1.4 times the volumetric efficiency and a maximum 80% reduction of maximum pressure rising rate are obtained. On top of that, the emissions in most of the operating range of the torque can be less than 100 parts per million and can be eliminated by post-treatment under stoichiometric combustion. The increase in cyclic variation is also small, less than 4.5%.

Although there are similarities between the knocking properties observed in hydrogen-powered WREs and reciprocating piston engines, it is important to note that hydrogen-fueled WREs exhibit distinct knock characteristics [7]. Consequently, specific measures must be implemented to mitigate knocking in these systems.

In addition, a study examines the efficiency of the Wankel rotary engine (WRE) fueled by hydrogen enriched with ammonia at varying levels of ammonia enrichment ( $\text{NH}_3\%$ ) and excess air ratios ( $\lambda$ ) [8]. Ammonia enrichment has been proven as a highly effective strategy to enhance the dynamic and economic performances of hydrogen-powered WRE. We observed that under specific operating conditions, such as 1200 r/min, a manifold absolute pressure (MAP) of 70 kPa, stoichiometric ratio, and maximum brake torque (MBT) ignition timing conditions, brake torque reached its peak at approximately 30%  $\text{NH}_3\%$ , while brake thermal efficiency peaked at around 40%  $\text{NH}_3\%$ . These findings indicate a significant improvement of 21% and 29%, respectively, compared to the use of pure hydrogen. However, it is important to note that ammonia enrichment does have drawbacks in terms of increased NO emissions. Notably, the optimal  $\text{NH}_3\%$  for hydrogen-fueled WRE differs significantly from what is required by hydrogen-fueled reciprocating piston engines

## 3 Historical evolution

To begin with, the German NSU vehicle manufacturer was the one who initially possessed the German Wankel engine proposed by Wankel [9]. On January 19, 1960, at a meeting of the German Engineers Association (VDI), which is prevalent today, it was Wankel who made it public that this invention of a new cutting-edge engine was imminent. This news soon reached the car manufacturing powerhouses, such as GM of the US, Daimler Benz of Germany, and Toyota of Japan. Nevertheless, it became apparent from the trial tests that the complexity of production would be more of a bomb than they had anticipated, and they all pulled out. To sign the patent license, Mazda agreed to vivid terms by focusing on getting the contract in February 1961.

In July of the same year, the organization dispatched an eight-member team of engineers, including Kenichi Yamamoto, to undertake a training course at the NSU factory in West Germany, and later, he was named the head of the RE Research Department. Then, for the demonstration, the engineers took a copper board off the Wankel engine while it was on, and the copper plate stood up as proof of the Wankel engine's quietness of operation. Still, after some time, they spotted that the lobes of the rotor vertex seal. That was the obstacle to mass production; after solving the problem, it would now go to the official phase. It was in 1963 (Showa 38) that Mazda set up the "RE Research Department" and appointed Kenichi Yamamoto to head 46 people to work on the Wankel engine for improvement. They would call them "Rotor 47 Warriors" like the "Ako 47 Ronin" if they did not succeed.

On the other hand, drumming the car industry as a result of passenger car liberalization, the Ministry of International Trade and Industry (now the Ministry of Economy, Trade and Industry) of Japan did not lag. It came up with the idea of merging the domestic automobile industry in 1965. Confronted with the current crisis and the possibility of such a merger, President Matsuda put forward the slogan "Technology is always innovative," which additionally spurred the RE Research Department.

Now, there are some engine types of peculiar to this one - for example, to say 'rotor reduces rotation' when the rotor (being a key component of the Wankel engine) moves in irregular oval orbits. It is important to construct the three faces employed at the three outlets of the cylinder so as to avoid diminishing airtightness between these faces and the cylinder wall by use of a three-dimensional linkage structure involving an apex seal and a corner seal. The apex seal function will follow that of the piston ring rings, and the metal spring sheet

will also expand and contract reciprocally between the wall and the apex seal gap spacing. When the sides of the top lose their geometry, they may be referred to as ‘devil’s fingernails’, which are the result of the cylinder wall being scratched by the apex seal. Put another way, the formula for enhancing the apex seal material performance is the development history of Wankel engines.

Once the tests were increasingly repeated, the researchers perceived a great similarity between the wear marks and the vibrations, which rise naturally above a certain point and become static, and so they changed the conformation of a so-called diamond seal. They created a diamond seal, which had a particular shape of a hole at its top and two spread-like holes going across, that were named cross-hollow diamond seals instead. The field trials confirmed that the pairs (diamond-cylinder) had experienced the frequency of resonance variation, thus not leaving any wavy wear marks within 300 hours and thus finalizing the initial problem. Then, by the summer of 1964, Japan Carbon Company (Japan Carbon Co., Ltd.) developed an alternative zinc to the first diamond seal fabricated by Mazda with a new aluminum alloy material mixed with carbon. The Cosmo Sport was unveiled in May of 1967 and assembled with a “carbon aluminum” material as it was to host a rotary power plant in a passenger production vehicle. ## 4 Conclusions

The usage of the Wankel Rotary Engine is in an embarrassing position nowadays since it consumes more fuel than other types of engines. Although it can do more work, it still needs to face the barrier of relatively low combustion efficiency. As technology advances and better fuels become available for the engine, the use of such engines could then be greatly expanded to more civilian vehicles.

The result of the experiment shows that a Wankel engine controls a lesser number of movable parts than the IC engine through the technology of fuel cells because many operational components are taken out but still obtain enough electrical yield. It is vital to have an engine like this, as when a conventional engine working in a car is taken as a case example, a part of the mechanical energy transferred to electrical power, an outcome of so many losses, is wasted. As a result, the Wankel rotary engine must eliminate its barriers of sealing, efficiency, and emissions mentioned in this paper to progress to the extent of becoming a widely used engine rather than an engine that still occupies a mere portion of the engine industry. The improvement in these three key areas, machine design, seal technology, and coat materials, as well as the advancing of novel alternative fuels, have addressed the challenges the Wankel technology had been facing, leading to the superior performance of the engine. The data lead to the prediction of a substantial lowering of oil consumption while the improved Wankel engine design is used, as opposed to the piston engines. Nevertheless, it should be noted that these optimized engines have a significant way to go in controlling hydrocarbon emissions.
